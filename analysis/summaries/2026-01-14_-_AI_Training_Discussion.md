## Key Points
- Colin Holmes sets context: insurance AI steerco was established mid-2025 at Diya/Mike's request to inventory AI activities, define 2026 priorities, and create an evaluation framework
- Three objectives for the steerco: (1) biggest bang for buck with AI initiatives, (2) evaluation framework with KPIs, (3) improved cross-functional working and skills consistency
- Previous training delivered by Pietro and Stephanie to insurance CS had 80 registered attendees -- described as a significant effort in a short window
- Pietro advocates a solution-focused approach: identify AI capabilities each project requires first, then map tools, then define learning -- training should be the last step, not the first
- Stephanie emphasises planning with "end in mind": define what success looks like for senior management, then work backwards to training design
- Stephanie proposes pre/post training surveys to measure knowledge transfer, plus tracking artefact creation (e.g., 25 of 30 attendees creating dashboards within 2 months)
- Azmain proposes bucketed training by outcome (dashboarding, presentations, etc.) rather than by tool -- this is a shift from previous tool-centric training
- Richard notes previous training was targeted at products/capabilities with use case examples; new approach flips to solution-focused, domain-specific content
- Train-the-trainer model suggested: identify SMEs within the business who can deliver training at scale, reducing dependency on Pietro/Stephanie
- Pietro and Stephanie's training content is on Moody's University as a playlist with recordings
- Colin asks about how training connects to the broader insurance operating model being developed with Maria Caramel in commercial strategy
- Discussion of training becoming "project number six" in the programme -- enabling all other workstreams

## Narrative Notes
This meeting is significant for two reasons. First, it formally establishes training as WS6 -- elevating it from an ad-hoc activity to a programme-level workstream with its own governance. Second, Pietro's intervention fundamentally reframes the training approach: instead of starting with "let's train people on Cursor/Claude/Copilot," start with "what capabilities do each of our five projects need?" then work backwards to tools and training. This is a more mature methodology that will produce better outcomes, but it also introduces a sequencing dependency -- the projects need to be well-defined before training can be designed, which could delay deployment. Colin's reference to AI objectives being written into performance reviews signals executive seriousness about adoption, but also raises the stakes for the training programme to deliver measurable results. The 80 registrations for previous training suggest appetite exists; the question is whether the new solution-focused approach can be operationalised quickly enough to maintain momentum.
