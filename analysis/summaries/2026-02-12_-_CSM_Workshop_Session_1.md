**Date:** 2026-02-12
**Attendees:** George Dyke (Speaker 1), Ronald (Speaker 2), Speaker 3 (European-based CSM), Miles (Speaker 5), Ashley (Speaker 6), Philip (Speaker 4), Rachel Gillespie (Speaker 7), Azmain Hossain, Natalia, Steve Gentilly, Liz Couchman, Thomas Harrison
**Duration context:** Long (multi-hour workshop, transcript covers ~50 minutes of substantive discussion)
**Workstreams touched:** WS1 Training, WS2 CLARA, WS4 Friday (Adoption Charter)

## Key Points
- This is Day 2 of a two-day in-person CSM workshop in London organised by George. The group reflects on Day 1 learnings before pivoting to IRP tooling and adoption squads.
- Communication gaps identified: CSMs were unaware that the sales team has restructured — property workflow specialists are now "relationship managers," there is a casualty relationship manager role, and cyber may move to the casualty side. This information only surfaced during a call with Ursula and Jennifer. The sales team does not send broad communications about structural changes.
- Ronald raises the need for better collaboration with the product team (not just sales). Model product managers have goals for new licences by HD model — CSMs were not aware of this. George agrees this is a gap and plans follow-up conversations with Lonnie, Maurizio, and Julie.
- Score adoption is presented as a case study by Miles. The adoption squad model is working: Katya (sales), Miles (CSM), Liz Couchman (solutions architect), Kristin Hilton (PM), Cherish Cricket (functional implementation lead), Emily Walton (technical), and Pratique Frank (analytical services). Kristin as PM has been instrumental.
- Score initially rejected the adoption charter (took 6 weeks to respond, then politely declined — they had their own plan). Lesson learned: having a solutions architect present at the initial charter conversation would have been better. The team pivoted to aligning their support with Score's own timeline rather than imposing the charter.
- Score asked for 600 vulnerability curves when offered 5-6. The aligned adoption squad was able to push back consistently with one voice because everyone was on the same page.
- Rachel Gillespie explains the implementation lead role: for non-consulting-bank clients, they offer two workshops plus two follow-up sessions. They are deliberately limiting free support to push clients toward implementation partners. This is a cultural shift.
- Thomas Harrison (model specialist lead) explains specialist team focus: getting clients onto HD models, RQD pipeline, financial model challenges on the reinsurance side.
- Introductions from Natalia (enablement and execution team, programme governance), Steve Gentilly (solution blueprints, solution fit analysis), and Liz Couchman (solutions architect, 7 years IRP experience).

## Risk Signals
- **Communication gaps between sales and CS are structural, not episodic.** The sales restructuring happening without CSM awareness is not a one-time miss — it reflects an ongoing disconnect. Any tool (CLARA, Sales Recon) that depends on alignment between sales and CS will be undermined by this gap.
- **Product team goals invisible to CSMs.** HD model licensing targets that CSMs do not know about means they cannot support sales conversations effectively. This is a missed revenue enablement opportunity.
- **Adoption charter rejection (Score).** The fact that a major client rejected the charter and had their own plan suggests the charter format may need rethinking — it should start from the client's plan, not impose a template.
- **Implementation boundary-setting is necessary but risky.** Rachel's approach (limited free support) could alienate clients who expect more from Moody's. The balance between scalability and client satisfaction is delicate.

## Narrative Notes
This workshop is the most valuable training and alignment activity the programme has produced — not because it teaches skills, but because it surfaces the organisational dysfunction that tools alone cannot fix. The sales restructuring communication gap, the invisible HD model licensing goals, the Score charter rejection — these are all symptoms of a company where functional silos operate independently and assume others are informed when they are not. George's achievement is creating a safe space for these issues to surface. The Score adoption squad case study is the best content of the session: it shows what works (aligned team with clear roles), what does not work (imposing a charter on a client who has their own plan), and how to adapt (pivot to the client's timeline). This is the kind of institutional knowledge that should be captured and replicated — and it would never have emerged without an in-person workshop.
