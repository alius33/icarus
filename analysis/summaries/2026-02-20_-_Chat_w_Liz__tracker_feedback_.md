## Key Points
- Meeting starts with ~5 minutes of political discussion (Prince Andrew, US politics) before pivoting to tracker work.
- Richard gives context: Christine has been thinking about acceptance criteria reporting, but Azmain reminds that adoption charter criteria already exist in CLARA, so the two should eventually converge.
- Azmain announces a feature development pause until after the Diya governance meeting on 25 Feb. Priority shifts entirely to preparing the presentation and getting executive sign-off.
- Liz provides the most substantive feedback of the week on adoption charter integration with CLARA. She introduces a clear taxonomy:
  - **Strategic objectives** -- the big-picture "why did they licence IRP" (e.g., one modelling platform, expense savings). Should sit prominently on each customer's front page in CLARA. Different from tactical activities.
  - **Target objectives** (per phase/use case) -- what CLARA currently calls "success criteria" but should be renamed for consistency with the adoption charter language.
- Liz uses AIG as a concrete example: four strategic objectives including "one modelling platform" and "expense savings," with target objectives per phase like "turn off Touchstone" and "HD-ready workflows."
- Liz confirms adoption charter templates are fully defined, actively in use, and have been established since October/November. There are two types: in-flight and pipeline. The 17-18 "accelerated" accounts have been identified as needing adoption charters.
- Azmain admits he did not know the adoption charter structure was already this well-defined -- a significant information gap that has delayed integration work.
- The group agrees on several quick wins: (1) Add a "strategic objectives" box to each customer's front page, (2) Rename "success criteria" to "target objectives," (3) Add adoption charter status field (not started / in progress / agreed) visible in portfolio reviews.
- Azmain raises the cultural challenge: some stakeholders (Kathryn Palkovics, Josh) are emotionally attached to the golden source Excel format and resist process changes. Liz volunteers to be added to the team chat to provide pushback -- she becomes a formal ally in the culture change effort.
- Liz mentions the CSMs are not doing adoption charters fast enough. Julia Valencia is one example: she has not started the Generali Risk Modeller charter and is trying to delegate it to Cherish, who has never worked on Generali -- a nonsensical handoff.
- Azmain describes using Copilot to read Teams messages and aggregate user feedback -- an example of his personal AI-augmented workflow.

## Narrative Notes
This meeting is where the adoption charter integration strategy crystallises. For weeks, the programme has been discussing how charters should relate to CLARA, but the conversations have been abstract. Liz Couchman changes that by arriving with concrete taxonomy (strategic objectives vs target objectives), real examples (AIG's four strategic objectives), and direct knowledge of what CSMs are and are not doing.

The most revealing moment is Azmain's admission that he did not know the charter structure was already defined. This is not a minor gap -- it is a symptom of the programme's fundamental communication problem. Azmain has been building features in isolation, responding to whoever shouts loudest, while parallel workstreams (Liz's charter work, Christine's acceptance criteria, Steve Gentilli's process) continue independently. The programme lacks a single place where all of this knowledge converges.

Liz's offer to join the team chat and provide pushback on golden source attachment is strategically valuable. Azmain and Richard have been fighting the cultural battle alone -- having someone with Liz's credibility and domain expertise as an ally changes the dynamic. She is not a developer or a programme manager; she is a solution architect who uses the tools daily and can speak with the authority of someone who lives the adoption process.

The naming convention decision (target objectives, not success criteria) may seem small, but it has outsized impact. When the tool's language matches the process language, CSMs stop being confused about what they are filling in and why. It is exactly the kind of detail that separates a tool people use from a tool people tolerate.

The Julia/Generali example is a microcosm of the broader CSM adoption challenge: even when templates exist and accounts are identified, CSMs are not doing the work, and when they try to delegate, they do it badly. The adoption charter status field in CLARA (not started / in progress / agreed) creates visibility that could drive accountability -- but only if leadership (Ben, Natalia, Josh) actually uses it to ask questions in portfolio reviews.
