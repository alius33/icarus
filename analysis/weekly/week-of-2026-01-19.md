## Executive Summary

Week 3 was defined by two parallel dramas: the race to stabilise CLARA before the most important meeting in the programme's life, and the expansion of the product's gravitational pull as new stakeholders began folding their separate tools into it.

The week opened in crisis. The deployed CLARA app on AWS was completely broken -- all API endpoints returning errors because Alembic database migrations had not propagated schema changes to the production RDS instance. BenVH was in Amsterdam racing an obstacle course and unreachable. Richard and Azmain spent two debugging sessions totalling ninety minutes on Sunday, using Claude Code to push fixes directly to GitHub -- a pragmatic but dangerous workaround that bypassed local testing entirely. The front-end was restored but the database layer remained broken until BenVH's return. This episode crystallised a pattern that had been building since Week 1: the team could build features at extraordinary speed (219 deployments and 105 commits from three people), but the deployment infrastructure was held together with duct tape and single-person dependencies.

The middle of the week was consumed by Monday preparation. The full cross-functional team -- including Idris from banking calling from an airport, Bernard and Alexandra from the Life team, George Dyke, and Martin Davies -- ran through three planned demos for the January 27 executive meeting with Colin Holmes, Ari LaHavi, and Mike Steele. Idris saw CLARA for the first time and immediately drew a parallel to banking's Credit Lens 360 Hub, endorsing the vision of CLARA as a central data source that feeds into other systems. Bernard demonstrated his Copilot Light sentiment dashboard for Sompo, which impressed everyone but drew sharp hallucination warnings from Idris based on direct banking experience. The strategic ask crystallised: either Sales Recon absorbs CLARA by end of FY26, or the team needs dedicated maintenance staff to sustain it independently.

Meanwhile, CLARA's data model became a convergence point. Steve Gentilli brought his standalone Excel adoption charter tracker and proposed consolidating it into CLARA -- 15 of his 20 columns already existed. Natalia shaped the portfolio review meeting format with surgical precision: high-priority accounts always, then reds by quarter, skip greens. Ben Brookes proposed a squad-based data entry model where CSMs coordinate with implementation leads, solution architects, and PMs rather than bearing the burden alone. The PMs received their first walkthrough of the data input hub, and Vlad offered to start testing immediately with three accounts. By Thursday, the team had agreed on a deployment freeze before Monday, a spec-first approach for schema changes requiring BenVH approval, and a deliberate choice of stability over new features.
