## Executive Summary

Week 5 was defined by the collision between the programme's ambitions and its operational reality. The first live Portfolio Review on Monday February 2 -- the watershed moment the team had been building toward for four weeks -- was overshadowed by a data loss incident. CSMs who had dutifully entered data over the weekend discovered their edits had vanished after a deployment refresh. George Dyke called for a show of hands to gauge the damage. Azmain promised publicly that it would not recur. Natalia held the meeting together despite the disruption, walking through nine priority accounts. The trust cost was real but the scope was limited: Richard managed Josh's alarm by narrowing the data loss to two affected account managers (Philip and Naveen). Despite the crisis, the structured approach itself was recognised as unprecedented -- the first data-driven portfolio review in the organisation's history.

The rest of Monday was consumed by RBAC debugging. The root cause of permissions failures was identified: Azure AD returns the username (e.g., "vanhouB") rather than the email alias, but the employee table stored aliases -- the system could never match users to permissions. Richard added optional claims to the Azure AD app registration while BenVH updated the backend code. Philip Garner served as a willing test subject, walking through browser dev tools to capture 403 errors. More ominously, Richard received a formal reprimand for using Cursor -- all subscriptions must be cancelled by February 10. His personal credit card was on Azmain's subscription. The Cursor compliance crisis had moved from budget inconvenience to professional risk.

The week's most strategically significant event was the cross-OU session with Amanda Fleming's KYC team on February 4. Amanda demonstrated a tool that eclipsed CLARA in feature richness -- client health dashboards, AI-generated tasks from meeting notes, contract parsing, renewal proposals, prospecting pipeline. But it had no authentication, was built for two users, and represented exactly the kind of ungoverned AI tooling that could trigger a corporate backlash. BenVH flagged the security gap. George Dyke asked the dangerous question: if Amanda can build this, what does Gainsight actually offer? The decision was correct -- no merging, compartmentalisation with shared data pipelines -- but Amanda's tool's existence raised the political stakes. Natalia delivered the week's most important intelligence: the Gainsight team was already asking "what the hell is Clara?" and had summoned Catherine to explain.

By week's end, Natalia had fundamentally reshaped CLARA's product direction. In a detailed requirements session, she redefined "priority" to mean the 31 accounts on the 2026 migration timeline aligned to the scorecard target, introduced a three-view architecture (Portfolio Review for operations, Management Dashboard for executives, CSM Manager View for people management), and established that client health RAG status must be manual CSM input, not algorithmically derived. Josh's feedback was relayed through Richard: action plans not populated (a UI bug, not a data gap) and duplicate product adoption rows from Salesforce imports. The team pushed back on Josh's implicit demand to make CLARA behave like Salesforce.
