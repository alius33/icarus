## Executive Summary

Week 6 was the programme's governance awakening. After five weeks of building at speed and dealing with the consequences, three events forced the programme to slow down and structure itself: Diya's first substantive engagement on AI programme governance, the CSM workshops in George's London team, and Josh Ellingson's defining declaration about CLARA's scope boundary.

Diya's meeting on February 11 was the week's most consequential fifteen minutes. For the first time, she engaged directly with the programme's strategic questions: what are the efficiency metrics? Is Sales Recon the biggest bang for the buck? How does AI bridge the gap from headcount reductions? She framed the discussion around ROI and asked for a "philosophical" alignment on priorities the following week. Idris claimed 15-30 hours per week savings per CSM -- a bold claim that set the measurement bar. Diya's lens was clear: AI investment must be justified by its ability to reduce the impact of layoffs on service quality. Richard offered to provide directional KPIs for each project, positioning himself as responsive rather than self-promoting.

On the same day, Josh drew the most important scope boundary since Ben Brookes's v1 decisions in January: CLARA is for managing IRP migration projects, not for day-to-day CSM work management. Gainsight will handle day-to-day work. This single declaration resolved months of ambiguity about where CLARA ends and Gainsight begins -- the exact question that had been identified as a risk in Week 2. Josh also revealed that Ben Brookes had promised Mike Steele and Colin Holmes access to CLARA, increasing visibility pressure. Data quality issues continued to surface: use case descriptions were wrong, duplicate products existed, and the fundamental data model needed expansion to track multiple products per use case, each with individual adoption status.

The CSM workshops in London on February 12 were the programme's first genuine user engagement at the practitioner level. George facilitated two sessions: a broader team discussion that surfaced the Score adoption squad as a reference model, and a dedicated CLARA hands-on session. In the hands-on session, Azmain made a public commitment that CSM-entered data would be preserved -- no more deployment wipes. He also flipped his approach from top-down (what do executives need?) to bottom-up (what helps the CSMs who enter the data?), a tactical shift informed by Richard's coaching. Asha asked the reciprocity question that every CSM was thinking: what do I get back from the data I put in? George's answer -- that Liz McLagan had stopped asking him for manual account updates and instead pointed to CLARA -- was the most tangible proof of adoption value.

Meanwhile, the Sales Recon pilot launched on February 11 with eight CSMs testing three CS-relevant features: Account Intelligence, Meeting Preparation Agent, and Share Knowledge Agent. The pilot would run through February 24 in the production environment.
