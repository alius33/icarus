## Executive Summary

Week 9 will be remembered not for what the programme built but for what it revealed about the people holding it together. Beneath a surface of genuine operational progress -- new team members producing, a new PM tool launched, CLARA shown to Life leadership, the Gainsight relationship formalised -- three personnel crises converged that threaten the programme's continuity more than any technical or political challenge to date.

The most consequential revelation came in fragments across three conversations. BenVH admitted to Azmain that his recent absence was not illness -- it was burnout triggered by Nikhil presenting App Factory architecture as his own, renaming it "Moplit," and receiving public credit in the advisory all-hands while BenVH's name went unmentioned. BenVH drew an explicit parallel to a traumatic Microsoft experience where a colleague took credit for his work and derailed his career. Hours later, Azmain told BenVH that Richard had been considering other positions -- driven by frustration with Rhett's uncoordinated work and his girlfriend's desire to relocate. And Azmain himself mentioned browsing the Moody's career website, wanting to move into product. The programme's three most essential people -- Richard for strategy and the Diya relationship, BenVH for all infrastructure and deployment, Azmain for everything else -- were all contemplating exit.

The operational layer, by contrast, was the programme's most productive week for new activity. Azmain revealed Friday, a full project management application built by thirty concurrent AI agents in a single day (burning his entire monthly Cursor budget). Diana endorsed it as the replacement for slide-based advisory project reviews. CLARA was demoed to the Life Side Senior Leadership Team, generating interest in the template/flat-pack approach for cross-OU reuse. Dan Flemington emerged as an unexpected ally from sales, bringing working Cursor-built tools and -- critically -- Julia Valencia, a contact with direct access to the Salesforce CDR database that had been a blocker for months. Catherine set up a formal Gainsight integration meeting and volunteered to serve as the governance gatekeeper for App Factory intake, transforming the programme's relationship with enterprise tooling from adversarial to collaborative. The migration dashboard was redesigned for Diya's scorecard needs, though a credibility risk around four pre-2026 completions being counted in 2026 targets remained unresolved.

The week's structural lesson was stark: the programme had scaled its ambition and its stakeholder footprint without scaling its governance or its care for the people doing the work. Documentation was zero. Credit attribution was informal. Personal financial expenditure on corporate tools was running at four hundred pounds per month between Richard and Azmain. The security team had caught them using personal Claude accounts to process proprietary data -- and Azmain openly admitted the defence of "no proprietary information" was false. These were not the problems of a programme that was failing. They were the problems of a programme that was succeeding faster than its institutions could support.
