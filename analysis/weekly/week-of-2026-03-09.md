## Executive Summary

Week 10 was defined by expansion — both in the programme's reach and in the gap between its ambition and its cost management infrastructure. Nine conversations across two days painted a picture of a programme whose reputation has outrun its resources: banking and life insurance both arrived asking for what the insurance team has built, Idris was formally onboarded as the first non-core-team member, Martin's Build in Five dashboard builder produced the kind of output that makes product teams nervous, and the sales team's data access requirements opened yet another front.

Three additional conversations from 9 March — before the main March 10 sessions — provided revealing supporting context. Azmain and Diana joined the advisory team's weekly project call for the first time, giving them (and us) visibility into the broader Charlotte-organisation workload that Diana manages. It is a staffing treadmill of cat bonds, ILS projects, and analyst allocation — a reminder that the Gen AI programme exists within a much larger operational machine that has its own pressures. Richard and Azmain's call with Juliet Valencia (sales analytics) advanced the Salesforce data access story: Dan Flemington's data likely comes from the SPM team's Tableau environment, not Juliet's Sales Hub, and Juliet will make the introduction once Dan confirms. After Juliet left the call, Richard's private sidebar with Azmain was strategically significant — he explicitly framed every cross-department engagement (Dan/sales, banking, asset management, Life, Singapore) as collectively building the business case for Diya to approve a dedicated team. And Azmain's deployment walkthrough with Chris revealed both the maturity of CLARA's infrastructure (939 tests per stage, three environments) and its growing pains (someone entering a 1999 date broke the burndown chart, CSMs still cannot use the app independently after four weeks).

The most impactful moment was Martin's demo in the Build in Five standup. What had been described as a drag-and-drop dashboard builder turned out to be a near-complete white-labelling platform with live Risk Modeller API integration, AI-assisted component generation, full theming, and save/load functionality. Richard's comparison to Databricks Genie — a capability the product team has but refuses to show customers — was telling. More importantly, Richard immediately mapped the strategic implication: the single biggest category of adoption blockers in CLARA is UI/UX problems, and Martin has built what amounts to a turnkey solution for that entire category. Ben Brooks saw it and began thinking about exceedance panel positioning rather than technical gaps. The stakeholder cascade was defined: MCP server integration, then tech consulting, then the demo team, then sales, then exceedance content production. If Martin can wire up the Navigator MCP server in the next two weeks, Build in Five goes from a concept to the programme's strongest adoption story.

The cross-OU expansion Diya had requested materialised in two parallel sessions. Banking's Gina Greer and Olivier described a team at the exact stage insurance was in November 2025 — siloed AI initiatives, no dedicated resources, tools without deployment solutions, and an executive (MENA) demanding immediate results. Life insurance's Jack Cheyne and Christian Curran learned about the native modelling engine and App Factory. Both conversations validated the programme's approach but also highlighted the uncomfortable truth: every new team that wants help means more demand on an already-overloaded infrastructure team. BenVH, who last week admitted his absence was triggered by Nikhil rather than illness, is now the deployment bottleneck for not just CLARA and Friday but potentially banking and life tools as well.

The cost infrastructure, meanwhile, is failing. AWS Bedrock has accumulated $1,163 in the first two weeks of March — on pace for $10,000 by month end. The shared API key has zero per-project or per-user cost attribution because the tags were never configured properly. Richard and Azmain's brief conversation about this was the week's most concerning exchange not for what was said but for what it revealed: the Bedrock migration solved the compliance problem of personal accounts but introduced a new problem of invisible, unaccountable cost scaling. When Richard mused about Max subscriptions as an alternative, the irony was clear — they might be going back to individual accounts because the corporate solution has no cost controls.

### WS2 CLARA — Stabilisation Continues
Chris's first bug fix emerged from the 9 March walkthrough with Azmain: date input fields across CLARA had no explicit min/max constraints, leaving them to browser defaults. Different browsers handled this differently, causing entry errors. More damaging, the absence of constraints allowed a user to enter a 1999 date that distorted the entire migration burndown chart. The fix — setting min to 2022 and explicit max — was agreed, with Azmain also deciding that the design intent is calendar-picker-only input (no free-form date typing). Chris raised a perceptive question about date format locale handling (US MM/DD vs European DD/MM) that Azmain had not considered. Chris is still blocked on full test suite execution because he lacks prod database access from BenVH. The deployment walkthrough itself was educational: Azmain showed Chris the three-environment pipeline (939 tests per stage) and the comparison to Cat Accelerate's Wild West approach highlighted how much infrastructure BenVH has quietly built. Azmain's frustration with CSM adoption reached its most explicit expression yet — after four weeks of the Portfolio Review being live, users still message him during the meeting asking where to click.
