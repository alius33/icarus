## Executive Summary

Week 10 was defined by expansion — both in the programme's reach and in the gap between its ambition and its cost management infrastructure. Six conversations on a single day painted a picture of a programme whose reputation has outrun its resources: banking and life insurance both arrived asking for what the insurance team has built, Idris was formally onboarded as the first non-core-team member, and Martin's Build in Five dashboard builder produced the kind of output that makes product teams nervous.

The most impactful moment was Martin's demo in the Build in Five standup. What had been described as a drag-and-drop dashboard builder turned out to be a near-complete white-labelling platform with live Risk Modeller API integration, AI-assisted component generation, full theming, and save/load functionality. Richard's comparison to Databricks Genie — a capability the product team has but refuses to show customers — was telling. More importantly, Richard immediately mapped the strategic implication: the single biggest category of adoption blockers in CLARA is UI/UX problems, and Martin has built what amounts to a turnkey solution for that entire category. Ben Brooks saw it and began thinking about exceedance panel positioning rather than technical gaps. The stakeholder cascade was defined: MCP server integration, then tech consulting, then the demo team, then sales, then exceedance content production. If Martin can wire up the Navigator MCP server in the next two weeks, Build in Five goes from a concept to the programme's strongest adoption story.

The cross-OU expansion Diya had requested materialised in two parallel sessions. Banking's Gina Greer and Olivier described a team at the exact stage insurance was in November 2025 — siloed AI initiatives, no dedicated resources, tools without deployment solutions, and an executive (MENA) demanding immediate results. Life insurance's Jack Cheyne and Christian Curran learned about the native modelling engine and App Factory. Both conversations validated the programme's approach but also highlighted the uncomfortable truth: every new team that wants help means more demand on an already-overloaded infrastructure team. BenVH, who last week admitted his absence was triggered by Nikhil rather than illness, is now the deployment bottleneck for not just CLARA and Friday but potentially banking and life tools as well.

The cost infrastructure, meanwhile, is failing. AWS Bedrock has accumulated $1,163 in the first two weeks of March — on pace for $10,000 by month end. The shared API key has zero per-project or per-user cost attribution because the tags were never configured properly. Richard and Azmain's brief conversation about this was the week's most concerning exchange not for what was said but for what it revealed: the Bedrock migration solved the compliance problem of personal accounts but introduced a new problem of invisible, unaccountable cost scaling. When Richard mused about Max subscriptions as an alternative, the irony was clear — they might be going back to individual accounts because the corporate solution has no cost controls.

## Workstream Progress

### WS6 Build in Five
The week's standout story. Martin's dashboard builder went from a concept that had been described as "drag-and-drop with three options per component" to a fully functional platform that could be mistaken for a commercial product. The demo revealed features nobody had anticipated: complete white-labelling with custom branding, live Risk Modeller API connections already working for the data sources component, an AI assistant mode (currently JavaScript-simulated, ready for MCP wiring), and save/load dashboard configurations.
