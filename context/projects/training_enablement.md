# WS1: Training & Enablement

## Overview

Training & Enablement is the first of six workstreams in the Customer Success Gen AI Programme. Its purpose is to upskill the insurance consulting and customer success teams in AI-powered tooling — not by teaching specific tools (Cursor, Copilot, etc.) but by building competency around solution-focused outcomes such as dashboarding, presentations, data analysis, and automation. The workstream was elevated to full workstream status at the December 2025 steerco, where senior leadership feedback indicated that training deserved dedicated focus rather than being a sub-task of another workstream.

The underlying philosophy, established in the 14 January planning session, is that training should be solution-focused rather than tool-focused — teaching people how to build dashboards rather than how to use Cursor specifically. This makes the skills transferable across whatever AI tools the organisation adopts.

## Training Framework (from 14 Jan Session)

The January 14 session with Colin Holmes, Pietro, and Stephanie produced a conceptual framework that has not been executed:

### Approach: Solution-Focused Buckets
Rather than training on tools (Cursor, Copilot, Claude), training should be organised around outcomes:
- **Dashboarding** — building data visualisations and management views
- **Presentations** — using AI to create and enhance presentations
- **Data analysis** — using AI to interrogate and transform data
- **Automation** — basic workflow automation and scripting

This approach was proposed by Azmain and endorsed by the advisory group. The rationale: tools change, but the ability to build dashboards or analyse data remains relevant regardless of which AI tool is used.

### Train-the-Trainer Model
Discussed as the scalable approach. Rather than Azmain or Ben personally training every CSM:
1. Identify a small group of "AI champions" or early adopters
2. Train them deeply
3. They cascade training to their teams
4. Provides ongoing support capacity without bottlenecking on one person

### Competency Assessment
Pietro advised a structured approach:
1. **Identify capabilities needed** per project/workstream — what AI skills does each role require?
2. **Assess current proficiency** — survey to establish baseline
3. **Design learning paths** — targeted training to close the gap
4. **Measure with KPIs** (Stephanie's emphasis) — clear success metrics, not just attendance numbers

### Cultural Context
The cultural challenge is significant. Ben described spending 90 minutes on the phone with Charlotte coaching her through basic Cursor usage. George's workshop revealed that some team members (e.g., ILS team) were not even using Microsoft Teams — far behind on basic tooling. The gap between early adopters (Ben, Azmain, George) and the rest of the team is wide.

## Blockers

1. **Azmain's bandwidth** — The single biggest blocker. Azmain is the nominal lead but has been 100% consumed by CLARA development since January. There is no one else driving this workstream forward.

2. **No dedicated owner** — Even if Azmain had bandwidth, the workstream needs someone with learning design expertise. Pietro and Stephanie are advisory — they consulted once (14 Jan) but are not executing.

3. **Tooling and licence constraints** — Cursor budget is limited ($20K corporate, shared across OUs). Opus 4.6 is 3x more expensive than previous models. Training sessions would consume significant tokens. Azmain and Richard are paying for Claude Code out of their own pockets (GBP 200/month each).

4. **No prioritisation mandate** — Diya has not explicitly prioritised training. The 23 Feb governance session established three programme pillars, none of which specifically called out training as an immediate priority.

5. **Cultural resistance** — Some team members are far behind on basic tooling adoption. The gap between early adopters and the mainstream is wide, requiring patience and repeated hands-on coaching rather than a single training session.

## Open Questions

1. **Who owns execution?** — Azmain cannot do this and CLARA. Someone needs to be assigned — potentially George Dyke (pragmatic, ran workshops), or a learning specialist from Pietro's team.

2. **What does success look like?** — Stephanie emphasised KPIs. No success metrics have been defined. Is it number of people using AI tools? Time saved? Apps built? Competency assessment scores?

3. **What format should training take?** — The December session had 50% attendance (40/80). Is classroom training the right model? Should it be one-on-one coaching (as Richard/Ben have been doing informally)? Should it be self-service materials?

4. **How does this relate to the broader Moody's AI steerco?** — Colin explained the insurance-wide AI steerco. Does WS1 need to align with or report into that governance structure? Are there enterprise-wide training resources that could be leveraged?

5. **Should Claude Code skills be part of training?** — The March 3 standup discussed Claude skills files and Nicole/Chris learning them. Richard mentioned building Moody's-specific skills as a future workstream. Does skills creation overlap with WS1?

6. **What is the relationship to the consulting AR platform?** — Richard mentioned (3 Mar) that building valuable skills for the team is part of "the consulting AR platform workstream that we haven't even started yet." Is this a sub-project of WS1 or a separate initiative?

7. **Budget for training delivery** — If training involves Cursor or Claude Code usage, each session consumes tokens. At current pricing ($750 in 3 days for one person), how is training token spend funded?
